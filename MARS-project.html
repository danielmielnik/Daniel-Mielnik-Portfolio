MARS Rocket Competition

DEC 2025 | Extracuricullar

Project Brief

As part of the Metropolitan Aerospace Rocketry Society (MARS), my five-person team, Yappasaurus Rex, designed, built, and launched YAREX, a model rocket powered by a G-class solid propellant engine. The process began with conceptual design in OpenRocket, where we refined the fin layout (inspired by the Saab 35 Draken) and optimized the nose cone for aerodynamics. We then manufactured the rocket using a reinforced cardboard body tube, 3D-printed nose cone, laser-cut balsa fins, and a custom avionics bay. Despite facing fitment issues with the avionics during competition day, we adapted quickly with on-the-spot fixes and successfully launched on December 20, 2024. The rocket reached a maximum altitude of 439.3 m, deployed its parachute correctly, and was safely recovered. Post-flight data analysis allowed us to compare actual performance with simulations, highlighting the effects of weather and drag on altitude and velocity predictions.

Process

Rocket Design

The design phase of YAREX began with aerodynamic modeling and stability analysis using OpenRocket. We focused on ensuring the rocket would achieve a safe and predictable flight while also incorporating unique design elements. The fins, inspired by the Saab 35 Draken’s double-delta wing, provided a stable center of pressure location without adding unnecessary mass. We iterated through multiple fin shapes before settling on this design, which offered both structural simplicity and aerodynamic stability. Similarly, the nose cone underwent a series of trial-and-error refinements before we finalized an ellipsoid shape. This design minimized drag while ensuring the weight remained low, an important factor in maximizing altitude.

Beyond these primary aerodynamic surfaces, we also paid close attention to the rocket’s internal balance. A transition tube was designed with added volume to house the avionics system, while also shifting the center of gravity upward to improve stability. This balance between center of gravity and center of pressure was carefully managed throughout the design process. CAD models of the avionics bay were created to check fitment and integration with the rocket body. These early design efforts ensured that when it came time to build the rocket, we had a well-thought-out plan that blended performance, functionality, and structural integrity.

Rocket Manufacturing

Once the design was locked in, we began manufacturing the rocket components. The body tube was fabricated from pre-cut cardboard reinforced with epoxy, ensuring durability while keeping weight low. The fins were precision laser-cut from balsa wood and carefully sanded to a tapered profile to reduce drag. After being slotted into the body tube, they were secured with epoxy fillets to withstand the stresses of flight. The nose cone was 3D printed and smoothed with sanding to achieve a snug fit and seamless aerodynamic transition with the main body. These steps gave us a sturdy, flight-ready airframe.

Attention then shifted to the recovery and avionics systems. The parachute system was assembled with a nylon parachute, Kevlar shock cord, and fireproof wadding to prevent damage during deployment. The avionics bay was designed as a modular unit housed within the transition tube, incorporating a PCB and GPS mount. While the bay was initially intended to be press-fit, we encountered issues with fitment tolerances, leading us to modify the mounting method later. For the finishing touches, we hand-painted the rocket with a metallic base coat, gradient coloring, and a reptilian scale pattern — a design that set YAREX apart visually while showcasing the team’s creativity and attention to detail.

Launch Day

Competition day presented both challenges and successes. During final assembly, we discovered that the avionics bay did not fit properly due to incorrect screw hole tolerances in the 3D print. To adapt, we improvised by securing the bay with screws and hot glue to stabilize the PCB and GPS. Additionally, the transition tube was too tight, requiring on-the-spot sanding to ensure it could separate correctly during ejection. These last-minute adjustments highlighted the importance of testing component fits thoroughly before launch and the value of being able to problem-solve quickly under pressure. Despite these obstacles, the team remained calm, collaborative, and efficient in resolving each issue.

After resolving the fitment problems, the rocket underwent safety inspections, which it passed without issue. We then carefully loaded the G131 motor, securing it with retention hardware, and double-checked the recovery system to ensure the parachute was packed correctly. With the rocket mounted on the rail, the ignition cables were attached, and the countdown began under the supervision of the Launch Control Officer. When the igniter was fired, YAREX roared to life, accelerating rapidly and reaching an apogee of 439.3 m. The ejection charge fired as intended, deploying the parachute at apogee and ensuring a smooth, controlled descent.

Watching the rocket return safely to the ground was a rewarding moment, confirming that all our hard work in design, manufacturing, and last-minute problem-solving had paid off. The rocket showed no structural damage upon recovery, and the avionics data provided valuable insight into the flight profile. Post-flight analysis revealed discrepancies between simulations and real performance, largely due to environmental conditions like wind and cold temperatures, which reduced motor efficiency and increased drag. Despite this, the flight was a complete success — both technically and as a learning experience in teamwork, resilience, and hands-on rocketry.

What I Learned

This project gave me valuable hands-on experience with the full rocketry design cycle; from CAD modeling and simulation to manufacturing, assembly, and launch operations. I learned how critical precision and tolerances are, especially in 3D-printed components like the avionics bay, where even small misalignments can create last-minute issues. I also gained experience in troubleshooting under pressure, adapting quickly with practical fixes to keep the rocket flight-ready. Working in a small, collaborative team of five reinforced the importance of sharing responsibilities and overlapping contributions to avoid gaps in the workflow. Finally, analyzing discrepancies between simulation and real-world data taught me the importance of accounting for environmental conditions like wind and temperature in aerospace design. Overall, the project strengthened my technical skills in design and manufacturing, while also sharpening my teamwork, adaptability, and problem-solving abilities.
