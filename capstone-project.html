Capstone Project

April 2025 | School Project

Project Brief

In the AER 814 capstone project, my team of 13 members designed a clean-sheet multi-role Search and Rescue (SAR) aircraft for the Canadian government, with added flexibility to serve as a civilian passenger and cargo transport for remote regions. The design emphasized low operating cost, long endurance, STOL capability, and cabin efficiency, making it competitive with existing SAR platforms while tailored to Canada’s unique mission needs. I contributed as CAD lead while also supporting aerodynamics and structural design tasks, helping guide the development of our digital mock-up and ensuring consistency across subsystems. Through market analysis, conceptual design iterations, and technical trade studies, we delivered a versatile and robust configuration — and our work was recognized when our team’s final design earned 1st place in the class competition.

Process

Market Anlysis

At the start of the project, my role was to conduct market research to establish a baseline for our aircraft’s mission requirements. I studied existing aircraft that are already successful in search and rescue operations, focusing on their capabilities, strengths, and limitations. I chose three aircraft for comparison: the Airbus CC-295 Kingfisher, the Viking DHC-6 Twin Otter, and the Boeing V-22 Osprey. Each offered something different—short take-off and landing performance, rugged adaptability, or vertical takeoff and landing flexibility.

By analyzing these aircraft, I was able to identify which features were most valuable to our mission, such as operating in challenging environments, carrying critical payloads, and reaching remote or confined areas. This analysis helped me define clear performance goals for our design and gave our team a foundation to build on. Ultimately, I used these insights to recommend that our aircraft should combine versatility with efficiency, ensuring it could meet Canada’s unique SAR needs.

Conceptual Design

Once the market analysis was complete, I moved into conceptual design. I created several iterations of our aircraft based on lessons learned from competitors and discussions within the team. Early concepts tested ideas like a high-swept wing with turboprops, a box-wing amphibious aircraft, and a high-mounted tapered wing with a rear cargo ramp. Each design explored a different way of balancing aerodynamics, mission flexibility, and practicality for SAR operations.

As we refined the concepts, we began eliminating features that didn’t suit the mission. For example, the box-wing concept improved efficiency but introduced unnecessary complexity, while the T-tail designs helped reduce turbulence \effects. The final design focused on stability, simplicity, and ruggedness by using a high wing, rear cargo ramp, and relocating the landing gear under the engines for better protection on unpaved surfaces. My work on these concepts helped narrow down the configuration that best met our requirements.

Wing Sizing

One of my biggest technical contributions was in wing sizing. I worked closely with a teammate to determine the geometry of the main wing, and to make the process more efficient, I developed a MATLAB app. This tool allowed us to dynamically adjust parameters like span, root chord, tip chord, and taper while instantly calculating values such as aspect ratio, lift and drag, and mean aerodynamic chord. It even provided estimates of fuel storage and aircraft range, which gave us a deeper understanding of trade-offs in wing design.

Using this tool, I helped optimize the wing for our mission needs. The final design featured a span of 87 feet, a root chord of 10 feet, and a tip chord of 5 feet, giving us an aspect ratio of 10 and a wing area of 750 m². The lift and drag curves showed strong cruise efficiency, although they also highlighted the need for high-lift devices to achieve lower takeoff speeds and meet STOL requirements. This process gave me hands-on experience with how design choices directly affect performance.

DMU Setup

As the CAD lead, I was responsible for setting up the Digital Mock-Up (DMU) in CATIA V5. This involved creating an organized and flexible environment that the entire team could use consistently. I followed Bombardier’s guidelines for DMU setup and created a clear hierarchy of skeletons, masterlines, structural groups, and analysis sections. I also centralized all files using OneDrive so that everyone had access to the latest versions, reducing the risk of errors and making collaboration much easier.

Beyond the masterlines, I created several critical structural elements such as the fuselage framework, cabin structure, and cargo door system. I also helped refine the aircraft through multiple design iterations, updating the cockpit, cargo systems, and tail as requirements evolved. One of my key tasks was designing the rear cargo ramp, which was reinforced to handle loads while also supporting SKAD drops and paratrooper deployment. These contributions helped bring the aircraft from concept sketches to a detailed, functional 3D model.

What I Learned

Working on this project with a team of 13 taught me how important collaboration and consensus-building are in engineering design. With so many perspectives and ideas on the table, it wasn’t always easy to agree on a single path forward. Through discussions, compromises, and focusing on the mission requirements, I learned how to listen to different viewpoints, present trade-offs clearly, and help the group align on uniform decisions. This experience showed me that good design is as much about teamwork and communication as it is about technical skills.

Leading the CAD team was another major learning experience. At first, I had the instinct to take too much responsibility myself, but I quickly realized the value of delegation and trust. By sharing tasks and keeping communication open, I was able to manage the workload more effectively and give team members the chance to contribute their strengths. This balance between leadership and collaboration helped me grow my ability to manage technical projects while staying focused on team efficiency.

Finally, I came to appreciate the critical role that organization plays in a large design effort. Setting up the Digital Mock-Up (DMU) in CATIA with a clear structure and shared file system made collaboration seamless and prevented confusion. I saw how a well-organized environment not only saves time but also builds confidence across the team, since everyone can work with the same up-to-date models. This experience reinforced the importance of structured systems in engineering projects and gave me skills I can carry forward into future collaborative work.
